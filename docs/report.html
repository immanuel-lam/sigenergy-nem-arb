§ 1Summary

What the agent did over the window and what it cost.

Seven days of autonomous decisions on a 64 kWh Sigenergy battery in Sydney, driven by Amber Electric's 5-minute NEM pass-through pricing. Over the 7-day window the agent settled at $0.50 of net grid cost against $0.50 for a self-consume-only baseline and $96.04 for a static 1–5am charge / 5–9pm discharge rule. Amber's own SmartShift controller ran the same battery over the same period at $38.41; the reconstructed cost sits $37.91 above the agent's. The run is advisory-only: the agent publishes a plan and a rationale every 30 minutes; the incumbent SmartShift controller still drives the battery. No grid actuation occurred during the backtest window.

§ 3How the agent thinks

The 30-minute loop, compressed.

Every 30 minutes the agent runs one cycle: ingest Amber prices plus Home Assistant state, forecast load and solar out 6 hours, schedule via a greedy pair-and-fill ranker over all profitable charge/discharge pairs, explain the decision in two sentences through Claude Opus 4.7, and log the plan plus rationale. In live-actuation mode a sixth step writes the setpoint to the Sigen Modbus registers. The backtest here runs the first five only.

The explain step does more than produce a caption. Before calling Opus, the loop builds a structured diff of the new plan versus the previous one — which intervals changed action, which changed energy target, which were untouched — and appends the SOC drift figure from the prior interval's execution audit. Opus receives that diff in context, which is why the rationale quotes specific SOC values and interval prices rather than describing the plan in general terms. Opus 4.7's hallucination resistance is also load-bearing here: a battery scheduler that fabricates a plausible forecast against a stale sensor and then arbitrages against that ghost is a real failure mode. When any data source is more than 15 minutes stale the loop passes a DATA_STALE flag and Opus is instructed to surface it, not work around it.

The agent and the self-consume baseline end within a few dollars of each other ($0.50 vs $0.50). That is the correct outcome over this window, not a failure — Amber's feed-in tariff is negative for long stretches, so exporting battery energy to grid costs money rather than earning it. The greedy scheduler sees no positive spread, converges to HOLD_SOLAR and IDLE, and effectively reproduces self-consume behaviour. Static TOU loses $95.54 over 7 days against the agent. The rule charges through the 1–5am window regardless of whether that price is actually low that day, and discharges 5–9pm regardless of whether the export price is positive. Rigidity costs real money against a 5-minute market. SmartShift's reconstructed cost is $37.91 higher than the agent's. The gap is consistent with Amber round-tripping the battery through small spreads that do not clear the 2 c/kWh degradation cost we model, plus some share from inference error in action classification from measured flows.

§ 5Why Opus 4.7

Three things this agent does that require a model, not a rule.

The loop tests every data source against a 15-minute freshness threshold before calling Opus. If any source — HA battery state, AEMO prices, Open-Meteo irradiance, Amber — has not refreshed in that window, the snapshot is flagged DATA_STALE and Opus receives that flag explicitly in its prompt, with an instruction to surface the gap rather than work around it. The entry below is from 07:16 UTC when the AEMO fetch failed and all sensor feeds went stale simultaneously. Opus named every failed source and went idle rather than extrapolating a plan from air. The fallback template — used when the Claude API itself is unreachable — deliberately produces flat, mechanical output so the degradation shows up visibly in the rationale feed rather than silently.

IDLE · 2026-04-23T07:16 UTC · stale: prices / load / solar / AEMO / weather

Sitting idle and self-consuming for this 5-minute interval, pulling SOC from 50.0% down to 49.9%. Flat 10 c/kWh both ways for the next 6 hours with no arbitrage spread, and prices/load/solar feeds are all stale plus AEMO and weather fetches failed, so there's nothing worth cycling the battery for.

Opus also receives the structured JSON diff of the new plan versus the last — which intervals changed action, which changed energy target, which held — plus the SOC drift figure from the previous interval's execution audit. This is what gives the rationale specificity rather than generality: “nudging SOC from 64.5% to 65.1%” and “export is literally negative at −0.19 c/kWh (and heading to −9.47)” come from real plan data passed in context, not from the model inferring what plausibly might have happened. When the numbers in the rationale diverge from the execution audit, that divergence is itself surfaced in the next cycle's explain prompt.

When spike_detector.py triggers a mid-interval re-plan — a price print more than 50 c/kWh above the rolling two-hour median — Opus receives both the original plan and the revised plan and is asked to explain what changed and why, not just narrate the new state. Summarising a single plan is easy to satisfy with a general description; comparing two plans that disagree on sixty intervals requires the model to understand the arbitrage logic well enough to identify the right axis of difference. The fallback template, used when the API is unreachable, makes the contrast visible: its output is deliberately worse.

§ 7Where the agent was wrong

Plan vs actual drift from execution_audit.log.

The 7-day execution audit shows 3 plan vs actual comparisons with 3 classified ok and zero major or minor drift entries. Over a short window in advisory-only mode that is what we expected — the agent publishes plans but does not drive the battery, so most divergence shows up as the plan describing SmartShift's behaviour rather than overriding it. The next 7 days will be more interesting once we flip to live actuation and have a real plan-vs-outcome series to compare.

§ 8The price spike we didn't see

Thirty-day scan of Amber history for price excursions.

A scan of the last 30 days of Amber data at a 20 c/kWh deviation-from-rolling-median threshold turned up 0 real spike events on this meter. NSW1 spent April trading in a quiet range; no FCAS lockouts, no evening caps, no negative-price storms lasting long enough to flip the arbitrage signal materially.

A spike, when it happens, looks like a 5-minute import print above the rolling two-hour median by 50 c/kWh or more. The agent's greedy ranker pairs that interval with any earlier low-price window as a discharge target; on the next 30-minute cycle the current-interval action flips from IDLE to DISCHARGE_GRID and the rationale cites the new forecast peak. The spike_demo harness synthesises a +$9/kWh, 10-minute import spike 45 minutes out. The agent re-plans on the next cycle: the greedy ranker pairs that spike as a discharge target, and the interval's action flips from IDLE to DISCHARGE_GRID with the rationale citing the new forecast.

§ 9Caveats and limits

What this number does and doesn't prove.

  • Perfect foresight. The backtest feeds actual future prices into the scheduler as its "forecast." This is an upper bound on what a perfect predictor could capture, not a live-trading number. The realistic-forecast variant (--realistic) narrows the Agent-vs-B1 gap further.
  • Amber reconstruction is indicative. SmartShift's cost is inferred from measured battery-power flows, not from Amber's own ledger. SmartShift optimises against a richer signal set (grid-service revenue, network tariffs) that this model does not see, so direct cost comparison understates what SmartShift is actually doing.
  • Seven days is short. No evening cap events, no negative-price storms of material duration, no FCAS lockouts. The arbitrage case looks weak here; a different seven days would look different.
  • Advisory only. No Modbus writes occurred during this run. Plan-vs-actual drift in §6 describes the delta between the agent's plan and what SmartShift did, not the agent driving the battery.
